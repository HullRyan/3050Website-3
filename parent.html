Computational thinking is the step that comes before programming. It’s the process of breaking down a problem into simple enough steps that even a computer would understand. We all know that computers take instructions very literally, sometimes to comic results. If we don’t provide computers with instructions that are precise and detailed, your algorithm might forget vital actions that most people take for granted. For example, consider a simple activity like brushing your teeth. At first it sounds like a simple enough task, but in fact, brushing your teeth involves many simple steps. First, you’ll need a toothbrush and toothpaste. You’ll need a sink with cold water. You’ll need to put the toothpaste on the brush. Don’t forget to turn on the water and run your brush underneath. As you see, such a simple activity actually involves many steps, if you miss one step or put one out of order you might end up with a huge mess! There are four major skills in computational thinking:

  • Decomposition
  • Pattern Recognition
  • Pattern Abstraction
  • Algorithm Design

Decomposition

Decomposition is the idea of breaking down a complex problem into smaller, easier to manage pieces. With children you can teach decomposition by getting them to teach you how to perform a simple task. Simple activities like brushing teeth, preparing food, or reading are great starters for understanding the process. Students will need to break down the task into small easy steps. Ensure that the steps are only ones that are required in order to complete the task. If in need of a challenge then another way to teach this concept is by having your student build something by only seeing the final product. Provide your student with the supplies needed and allow them to figure out the steps required to complete the project. With this activity you can understand the basics of decomposition.

Pattern Recognition

People are generally already good at recognizing patterns, however it is still vital when trying to understand computational thinking. Pattern recognition is all about identifying a repeated pattern or problem and considering if any solutions encountered in the past apply here. One practical way to think about this is by comparing it to building a piece of furniture from IKEA. Something like a set of drawers requires that each drawer be built individually, when building the first one it's common to make a couple mistakes and to take a long time. As you continue to build the identical drawers you gain an understanding of the process that is required and avoid the mistakes made in the past. When we repeat steps in the build we learn to solve the instructions more quickly and efficiently.

Pattern generalization and abstraction

Pattern generalization helps to teach about identifying relevant details needed in solving a problem and disregarding details that are not important to the task at hand. The process of identifying crucial info and ignoring irrelevant data is one of the hardest parts of computational learning. One common and popular example of pattern generalization are escape rooms. Many escape rooms contain irrelevant details and props that aim to distract from the puzzle needed to be solved. Setting up an escape room at home can be a great way to teach the concept of pattern generalization.

Algorithm design

While an algorithm sounds complicated and technical, it is at its most basic just a process or set of rules to be followed. Algorithm design is identifying and determining the steps and rules needed to be followed in order to achieve the same outcome every time. One simple example for this is to have you and your student each write down a set of instructions for creating something, for example a peanut butter and jelly sandwich. Then trade your instructions and attempt to follow the others instructions exactly. Doing this you may find that some steps like putting the peanut butter and jelly away may or may not have been included, steps like these can be very crucial when determining a good algorithm.

Trying some of these out with your student can give them a sense of what computational thinking is and how it can be applied, many free online resources are available for expanding upon your computational thinking skills, some of our favorites are listed on this website.
